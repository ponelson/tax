Capital Gains & NIIT

0 · 15 · 20 + 3.8%

Hold more than a year and gains get their own preferential 0/15/20% ladder — far below ordinary rates. Hold a year or less and they're taxed like wages. Above the income lines, a 3.8% surtax stacks on top.

2026 long-term brackets (taxable income): 0% up to $49,450 single / $98,900 joint; 15% to $545,500 / $613,700; 20% above. The 3.8% NIIT hits net investment income once MAGI tops $200K single / $250K joint — and those thresholds are frozen (never indexed), so more people cross them every year. A 20%-bracket gain effectively costs 23.8% federal, plus state. Losses offset gains first, then up to $3,000 against ordinary income, with the rest carried forward — the basis of tax-loss harvesting. Qualified dividends ride the same 0/15/20% ladder.

№ 03

Traditional vs. Roth

THE CORE DECISION

The whole question in one line: is your tax rate lower now or lower later? Traditional deducts today and taxes the withdrawal; Roth taxes today and never again. Same limits, opposite timing.

You have a fixed amount of pre-tax income to save this year. Compare funding a Traditional account (deduct now, taxed at withdrawal) against a Roth (no deduction, tax-free at withdrawal) — with the Traditional tax savings invested alongside for a fair fight.

In theory, an identical rate now and later makes the two tie exactly — the math is symmetric. In practice the Traditional side pot (the invested tax deduction) sits in a taxable account and loses a little to capital-gains drag, which this model reflects. Roth wins clearly when your future rate is higher, and offers no lifetime RMDs and better treatment for heirs; Traditional wins when you'll drop into a lower bracket in retirement.

№ 05

The Taxable Brokerage

THE FOURTH BUCKET

The account with no limits, no lockup, and no withdrawal rules — and quietly, real tax advantages: preferential capital-gains rates, tax-loss harvesting, and a full step-up in basis at death that can erase a lifetime of gains for your heirs.

Compare $1 of growth in a taxable account against a tax-deferred one over time. The taxable account pays a small annual drag on dividends but wins its gains at capital-gains rates — and if held until death, the basis resets and the gain is never taxed at all.

The step-up is the taxable account's hidden superpower: heirs inherit at market value, so the entire lifetime gain escapes income tax (it may still face estate tax above the exemption — that's what the estate vehicles below address). This is the "buy, borrow, die" logic in one line. Live drawbacks: annual tax on dividends and any realized gains, no deferral on the way in. Best used as the flexible bridge account and the place to hold your most-appreciated, most-likely-to-bequeath positions.

Social Security & Drawdown Order

THE INCOME PUZZLE

How much of your Social Security is taxed depends on your other income — up to 85% gets pulled in above the thresholds. Which accounts you spend from, and in what order, quietly decides your lifetime tax bill.

Provisional income = other income + tax-exempt interest + half your benefit. Thresholds (frozen since the 1980s–90s): 50% tier at $25K single / $32K joint, 85% tier at $34K / $44K. The classic drawdown order to smooth lifetime tax: spend taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred, then Roth last — while doing Roth conversions in the low-income gap years. Note muni interest still counts here, so "tax-free" bonds aren't free for this test. QCDs (up to $108K in 2026) and the temporary $6,000 senior deduction can both ease the squeeze.

The 529-to-Roth rollover has real guardrails: the account must be 15+ years old, only funds seasoned 5+ years qualify, it's capped at $35,000 lifetime per beneficiary, each year's rollover counts against that year's Roth limit, and the beneficiary needs earned income. DAF

Take the full deduction today. Grant the money later, on your own clock, with the assets compounding tax-free in between.

№ 10

Charitable Remainder Trust

CRT

Sell a concentrated, low-basis position without paying capital gains. Receive an income stream for life or a term. Remainder goes to charity.

You hold appreciated stock and want to diversify without absorbing the tax hit. The trust sells it, reinvests, and pays you.

Capital gains tax avoided at funding $1,071,000
Year-1 payment to you $250,000
Cumulative payments over term $5,000,000
Estimated charitable remainder $3,386,355

The 10% remainder-interest rule is the cliff: the PV of what charity ultimately receives must be at least 10% of the contribution at funding. Push the payout too high and the IRS disqualifies the trust.

Charitable Lead Trust

CLT

The mirror image of a CRT — charity gets the annuity stream now, your heirs get the remainder. Best when the §7520 hurdle rate is low.

№ 12

Grantor Retained Annuity Trust

GRAT

A short-term bet on appreciation. You get an annuity stream back equal to your contribution; anything above the IRS hurdle passes gift-tax-free.

You move a pre-catalyst asset (concentrated stock, pre-IPO equity) into a 2-year zeroed-out GRAT.

Annual annuity back to you $5,378,049
Exemption used $0
Passes to heirs gift-tax-free $3,409,073

"Zeroed-out" means the PV of what comes back to you equals what went in — the gift, on paper, is zero. If the asset underperforms the hurdle, nothing transfers and you've lost only transaction cost. If you die during the term, assets snap back to your estate.

№ 13

Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust

IDGT

"Defective" for income tax (you keep paying it) but effective for estate tax. Sell appreciating assets to it on a note. Growth above AFR escapes your estate.

After a 10% seed gift, you sell $10M of an appreciating asset to the trust for a 9-year balloon note at the AFR.

Seed gift (exemption used) $1,000,000
Asset value at note maturity $23,579,477
Out-of-estate transfer to heirs $8,718,083

The income tax you keep paying on trust earnings is itself a transfer-tax-free gift — a "tax burn" that bleeds your taxable estate while letting the trust compound on a gross basis. Quiet, mechanical, devastating to the estate-tax bill.

№ 14

Spousal Lifetime Access Trust

SLAT

Lock in today's exemption while preserving indirect access through your spouse. The workhorse vehicle for HNW couples in 2026.

You use your full $15M exemption to fund a SLAT for the benefit of your spouse and descendants. Assets grow outside your estate.

№ 15

Qualified Personal Residence Trust

QPRT

Transfer a residence at a discounted gift value by retaining the right to live in it for a term of years.

№ 16

Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust

ILIT

Hold a life insurance policy outside your estate. The death benefit funds liquidity for estate tax — without itself being taxed.

Your estate is illiquid (operating business, real estate). The ILIT-owned policy generates cash to pay the estate tax bill without forcing a fire sale.

Total premiums paid $3,000,000
Estate tax avoided on death benefit $4,000,000
Net liquidity created for heirs $7,000,000

Without the ILIT, a $10M death benefit lands inside the estate and gets taxed at 40%. With it, the full benefit passes intact — and arrives as cash, exactly when the IRS comes calling.

GST-EXEMPT

FLOW

Where Should the Next Dollar Go?

PRIORITY ORDER

A rough consensus waterfall for a saved dollar. Not gospel — your situation reorders it — but a sane default that captures free money first and the best tax treatment next.

1

401(k) up to the employer match

A 50–100% instant return. Always first — never leave the match on the table.

2

Pay off high-interest debt

A guaranteed, tax-free return equal to the interest rate. Credit-card debt beats any investment.

3

Max the HSA (if eligible)

The only triple-tax-free account — deduct in, grow free, spend free on medical. A stealth retirement account.

4

Max IRA / Roth IRA

Roth if you're below the income limit; backdoor Roth if above. Broad, cheap investment menu.

5

Finish maxing the 401(k)

Up to $24,500 (2026). Add the mega-backdoor Roth here if your plan allows after-tax contributions.

6

529 for education goals

If you have children and a state deduction, fund it — leftover money can later roll to their Roth.

7

Taxable brokerage

Unlimited, flexible, capital-gains rates, and step-up at death. The catch-all for everything above.

Reorder for your reality: no employer plan skips step 1; a big near-term expense may pull the brokerage up; a high earner already phased out of Roth leans on backdoor and mega-backdoor. The point isn't the exact sequence — it's capturing free money and tax-advantaged space before taxable dollars.

RULES

The Rules That Bite

KNOW THESE

The calculators above assume you clear the fine print. These are the rules that most often trip people up — the deadlines, penalties, and clocks that quietly decide whether a strategy works.

RMDs

Required Minimum Distributions

Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s force taxable withdrawals starting at age 73 (75 for those born 1960+). Miss one and the penalty is 25% of the shortfall (10% if fixed quickly). Roth IRAs have no lifetime RMDs — a core reason to hold Roth late in life.

5-YEAR

The Roth 5-Year Rule

Earnings come out tax-free only after the account is 5 years old and you're 59½. Each Roth conversion starts its own 5-year clock for penalty-free access to the converted principal. Contributions themselves can always be withdrawn tax- and penalty-free.

PENALTY

Early Withdrawal (before 59½)

A 10% penalty plus ordinary tax on most pre-59½ withdrawals. Exceptions: first home ($10K), qualified education, birth/adoption ($5K), disability, substantial medical, and 72(t) equal payments. The 401(k) "rule of 55" allows penalty-free access if you leave that job at 55+.

PRO-RATA

The Pro-Rata Rule

The silent killer of naive backdoor Roths. If you hold any pre-tax IRA money, a conversion is taxed proportionally across all your IRAs — you can't cherry-pick the after-tax dollars. Clear the decks (roll pre-tax IRAs into a 401(k)) before converting.

STEP-UP

Step-Up in Basis at Death

Appreciated assets held until death get their cost basis reset to market value — heirs owe no capital gains on the prior appreciation. This is why some low-basis assets are best held, not gifted, and it interacts directly with the estate vehicles below.

ROTH C-U

Mandatory Roth Catch-Up (2026)

New under SECURE 2.0: if your prior-year wages exceeded $150,000, your 50+ catch-up contributions must go into a Roth (after-tax). If your plan offers no Roth option, you may lose catch-up eligibility entirely — check with your administrator.

SALT

SALT Deduction Cap

State and local taxes are federally deductible only up to $40,400 in 2026, phasing down above ~$505K MAGI to a $10K floor. Pass-through owners can often route around it via a state PTET election — modeled in section 01.

GIFT

Annual Gift Exclusion

You can give $19,000 per recipient in 2026 with no gift-tax filing and no use of your lifetime exemption. 529s allow 5-year front-loading — up to $95,000 at once per beneficiary ($190,000 per couple) — treated as five years of exclusions.

Deadlines matter as much as rules: IRA and HSA contributions for a tax year run until the April filing deadline; 401(k) elective deferrals must be made by December 31. Contribution limits and thresholds are indexed and shift annually — these reflect 2026.

TERMS

Glossary

PLAIN ENGLISH

The acronyms and terms this guide leans on, in one place.

AGI
Adjusted Gross Income — total income minus specific "above-the-line" deductions. The starting point for most tax math.
MAGI
Modified AGI — AGI with certain items added back. Governs phase-outs: Roth eligibility, NIIT, IRMAA, and more.
Taxable income
AGI minus your standard or itemized deduction. This is the figure the tax brackets actually apply to — not gross income.
Marginal vs. effective rate
Marginal is the rate on your last dollar; effective is total tax ÷ total income. Effective is always the lower number.
Cost basis
What you paid for an asset. Gain = sale price − basis. Resets to market value at death (the "step-up").
NIIT
Net Investment Income Tax — a 3.8% surtax on investment income above $200K/$250K MAGI. Thresholds are frozen.
RMD
Required Minimum Distribution — the forced, taxable withdrawal from Traditional accounts starting at age 73.
QCD
Qualified Charitable Distribution — a direct IRA-to-charity gift (up to $108K in 2026) that satisfies your RMD tax-free.
Provisional income
Other income + tax-exempt interest + half your Social Security. Decides how much of your benefit is taxed.
PTET
Pass-Through Entity Tax — a state election that routes state tax through a business to sidestep the federal SALT cap.
SALT
State And Local Tax — the itemized deduction for those taxes, capped at $40,400 in 2026 with a high-income phase-down.
AFR / §7520 rate
IRS-published interest rates (the §7520 is the "hurdle") used to value annuity, life, and remainder interests in trusts.
GST
Generation-Skipping Transfer tax — a separate 40% tax on gifts that skip a generation; has its own exemption.
Backdoor Roth
Contribute to a non-deductible IRA, then convert to Roth — a legal path around the Roth income limit. Mind the pro-rata rule.
Step-up in basis
At death, an asset's basis resets to market value, erasing the income tax on all prior appreciation for heirs.
IRMAA
Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount — a Medicare premium surcharge triggered by high MAGI two years prior.

Every dollar figure and threshold in this guide reflects 2026 and is indexed or frozen as noted. Terms are simplified for intuition — the Internal Revenue Code defines each with far more precision.
